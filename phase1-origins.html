Origins

The First Sacred Enclosure

Neolithic Period · c. 3000 BCE

The Birth of a Sacred Circle

Around 3000 BCE, Neolithic communities living on the Wiltshire plain began digging a roughly circular ditch — nearly 110 metres in diameter — using tools made from deer antler and ox shoulder blades. The earth removed from the ditch was piled inward to form a continuous bank, creating a clearly defined, enclosed ritual space that would serve as the foundation for everything that followed.

Inside the circular earthwork, archaeologists have identified 56 small pits known as the Aubrey Holes, named after the 17th-century antiquarian John Aubrey who first described them. These pits were arranged in a precise circle and are believed to have originally held upright wooden posts or small stones. Over time, they were also used as repositories for cremated human remains — making Stonehenge one of the largest prehistoric cremation cemeteries ever found in Britain.

The northeast entrance was deliberately oriented toward the midsummer sunrise, suggesting the site was already conceived as an astronomically aware monument. Timber posts were also erected in the entrance causeway — possibly acting as markers for celestial events. This early phase lasted for centuries, with the site functioning as a gathering place for ritual, ancestor veneration, and communal ceremony, long before a single stone was raised.
