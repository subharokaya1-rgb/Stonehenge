Phase III · c. 2200 BCE

The Great Monument

Sarsen Trilithons & Solar Alignment

Bronze Age · c. 2200 BCE

Engineering a Monument for Eternity

Around 2200 BCE, the people of the early Bronze Age undertook the most ambitious construction phase in Stonehenge's long history. Enormous blocks of sarsen sandstone — sourced from Marlborough Downs roughly 25 kilometres to the north — were dragged to the site on wooden sledges and rollers pulled by hundreds of workers. The largest stones weigh up to 25 tonnes and stand over 7 metres tall.

These sarsen uprights were topped by horizontal capstones called lintels, forming the iconic paired stone gateways known as trilithons (from the Greek for "three stones"). The lintels were secured using mortise-and-tenon joints — carved stone knobs that lock into corresponding hollows — a technique borrowed from woodworking and adapted brilliantly for stone construction. The curved inner face of each lintel was shaped using a technique of gentle flaking so they would form a continuous arch when placed in a ring.

The outer sarsen circle and the inner trilithon horseshoe arrangement were aligned with extraordinary precision. At midsummer, the rising sun shines directly through the main northeast entrance and down the central axis toward the Altar Stone. At midwinter, the opposite effect occurs — the setting sun aligns perfectly with the great central trilithon. This solar connection was not accidental: it was a deliberate statement about time, the cosmos, and the cycle of life and death.
