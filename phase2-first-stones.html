Phase II · c. 2500 BCE

First Stones

The Arrival of the Bluestones

Chalcolithic Period · c. 2500 BCE

Stones Carried Across a Continent

Around 2500 BCE, something extraordinary happened: a group of standing stones known as bluestones were transported from the Preseli Hills in Pembrokeshire, Wales — a distance of over 240 kilometres — and erected within the existing earthwork enclosure at Stonehenge. This represents one of the most remarkable feats of prehistoric logistics in the ancient world.

The bluestones weigh between 2 and 5 tonnes each, and archaeologists believe they were moved using a combination of wooden sledges, rollers, rafts, and human muscle. Some researchers propose a coastal sea route around the south of Wales, while others argue for a more direct overland path. Either way, the effort required would have demanded enormous social organisation and communal determination — suggesting the stones carried deep cultural or spiritual significance to the people who transported them.

One prevailing theory is that the bluestones had long been venerated in Wales before their relocation. Some geologists and archaeologists suggest the stones were associated with healing powers or ancestral authority, perhaps because they came from a place regarded as sacred. Their specific origins — the outcrops at Carn Goedog and Rhosyfelin — have been identified through precise geochemical analysis, confirming the astonishing precision of prehistoric stone-sourcing. Once at Stonehenge, the bluestones were arranged in what appears to have been an incomplete double-arc or circle before the great sarsen phase began.
