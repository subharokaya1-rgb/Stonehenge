Archaeological Record

Five Phases of Construction

Over 1,500 years of ritual activity, stone-moving, and astronomical alignment shaped the monument we see today.

Phase I

Origins

c. 3000 BCE

A circular earthwork enclosure was dug by hand with deer-antler picks. The ditch, bank, and 56 Aubrey Holes formed the first sacred space — already used for cremation burials and seasonal ritual gatherings.

Explore Phase →

Phase II

First Stones

c. 2500 BCE

Bluestones from the Preseli Hills of Wales — over 240 km away — were transported and erected. Their arrival marks one of the greatest feats of prehistoric logistics, possibly tied to beliefs in healing or ancestral power.

Explore Phase →

Phase III

Great Monument

c. 2200 BCE

Massive sarsen sandstone blocks — some weighing 25 tonnes — were raised as towering trilithons with lintels secured by mortise-and-tenon joints. The horseshoe arrangement aligns perfectly with the summer solstice sunrise.

Explore Phase →

Phase IV

Modifications

c. 1500 BCE

Bluestones were rearranged into a new inner circle and horseshoe. The ceremonial Avenue was extended to the River Avon, creating a processional journey that connected the living and the dead across the landscape.

Explore Phase →

Phase V

Present Day

2026 CE

A UNESCO World Heritage Site drawing over 1.5 million visitors annually, Stonehenge remains under active archaeological investigation. Modern conservation and a new visitor centre protect its legacy for future generations.
