World Heritage & Living Legacy

Modern Era · 1986 CE – Present

Ancient Wonder in the Modern World

In 1986, Stonehenge was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site — a recognition of its outstanding universal value as one of humanity's most important prehistoric monuments. Together with Avebury and the surrounding landscape, it forms part of a protected archaeological area of extraordinary richness and density. Today, Stonehenge is managed by English Heritage, which oversees conservation, access, and public education.

The site receives over 1.5 million visitors each year, making it one of the most visited prehistoric monuments on Earth. In 2013, a landmark new Visitor Centre was opened 2.8 kilometres from the stones, dramatically improving the visitor experience while reducing the footprint of tourism on the ancient site itself. The old roads and car parks that once surrounded the stones have been removed, restoring some of the monument's historic setting. Visitors now arrive by shuttle bus or on foot along a pathway that echoes the experience of ancient processional approach.

Archaeological investigation continues to transform our understanding. The Stonehenge Hidden Landscapes Project used ground-penetrating radar, magnetometry, and other non-invasive techniques to reveal dozens of previously unknown monuments beneath the fields surrounding Stonehenge — including buried pits, long barrows, and the remains of a massive timber circle. DNA analysis of ancient remains has revealed the population movements behind Stonehenge's builders. New geochemical sourcing confirms the exact Welsh quarries of the bluestones. And debates continue about the monument's precise function — solar temple, healing centre, ancestral cemetery, or all three simultaneously. Stonehenge remains one of the world's great unsolved mysteries, and its cultural significance continues to grow with every season of discovery.
