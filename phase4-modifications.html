Phase IV · c. 1500 BCE

Modifications

The Avenue & Ritual Landscape

Middle Bronze Age · c. 1500 BCE

Rearranging Stones, Reshaping Meaning

The centuries between 2000 and 1500 BCE saw significant changes at Stonehenge as its builders and custodians adapted the monument to new ritual and social needs. Chief among these changes was the rearrangement of the bluestones into their current positions: an inner oval (later a horseshoe) and an outer circle nestled between the great sarsen uprights. This meticulous repositioning shows that the bluestones had lost none of their sacred importance — they were too precious to remove, only to be carefully re-set in new configurations.

During this phase, the builders also completed and extended the Avenue — a pair of parallel earthwork banks and ditches stretching nearly 3 kilometres northeast from Stonehenge before curving southeast to the River Avon. This was not merely a path: it was a processional route of enormous ritual significance. LiDAR survey and geological analysis have shown that the Avenue follows a natural ridgeline whose underlying chalk geology was scoured into parallel grooves by melting glaciers during the last Ice Age — grooves that happen to align perfectly with the midsummer sunrise and midwinter sunset.

Archaeologists believe that mourners or pilgrims would travel by boat along the River Avon and then walk the Avenue to reach the monument — a journey that symbolically moved between the realm of the living and the domain of ancestors. The river, flowing between Durrington Walls (a massive settlement site) and Stonehenge, formed the backbone of a wider ritual landscape in which the monument was not isolated, but the culminating destination of a sacred, processional experience.
